Last Rites

Scroll

‍

The last rites of the Catholic Church focus on preparing the soul of the dying person for their final journey to eternal life. While the Sacrament of Anointing of the Sick is often mistakenly associated with the last rites, it is the Eucharist, known as Viaticum or “food for the journey,” that is the true sacrament for those approaching death. Viaticum is the final sacrament of Christian life, signifying the soul’s passage through death to eternal life. It is both the culmination and the crown of a life lived in Christ, reflecting the hope of the heavenly banquet promised by the Lord.

As Jesus said, “Whoever eats my flesh and drinks my blood has eternal life, and I will raise them up on the last day” (John 6:54). Viaticum unites the dying person to Christ in a profound way, strengthening them to follow Him into eternal glory. This sacred moment acknowledges the Christian’s lifelong journey of discipleship and their trust in God’s promise of eternal life.

Accompanying the Eucharist, the prayers of commendation surround the dying person with the love and support of their family, friends, and the Church. These prayers gently entrust the individual to God’s loving care, affirming the hope that they are entering into the fullness of His mercy and peace. In these moments, the community joins in spirit with the dying person, echoing the words of Christ on the cross: “Father, into your hands I commend my spirit” (Luke 23:46).

In exceptional circumstances—such as a sudden health crisis or accident—if a priest is available, a rite combining the sacraments of Penance, Anointing of the Sick, and Viaticum may be celebrated. This powerful liturgical moment provides reconciliation, healing, and spiritual nourishment for the journey to eternal life. However, the Church teaches that sacraments cannot be administered to someone who has already died, as they are intended for the living to receive God’s grace.

The parish community of Sts Peter and Paul is committed to offering spiritual and pastoral support during these difficult times. Families are encouraged to contact the parish office when a loved one is nearing death, so that the Church can accompany them with prayer, sacraments, and compassion. Let us entrust our loved ones to the mercy of God, who “wipes away every tear” and promises a life where “death will be no more” (Revelation 21:4).
